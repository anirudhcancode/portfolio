The Origin

What it started as

Every quarter, every public company publishes an earnings report — a detailed breakdown of their financial performance. Analysts and investors need to read hundreds of these reports to make decisions. Each report can be 50 pages or an hour-long call transcript. The goal was simple: automate that.

1

Ingest earnings call transcripts

Real transcripts from 5 major companies — Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, and NVIDIA — stored in PostgreSQL.

2

Summarize with BART

Facebook's BART model condenses each 10,000-word transcript into a plain English summary in seconds. No human reading required.

3

Analyze sentiment with FinBERT

FinBERT — a BERT model fine-tuned specifically on financial text — classifies each report as positive, negative, or neutral with a confidence score. Unlike general sentiment models, FinBERT understands financial language: "flat revenue" isn't neutral in finance, it means no growth.

4

Extract keywords with YAKE

Statistical keyword extraction identifies the most important topics discussed — no GPU or model required.

5

Serve via FastAPI

All results exposed via REST endpoints — summaries, sentiment scores, keywords, and per-ticker queries.

Results

What the models found

FinBERT correctly identified Apple as the only negative report at 94.88% confidence — picking up on the 13% China revenue decline and 25% iPad revenue drop that drove the negative tone. All other four companies were correctly identified as positive.

Company Sentiment Confidence Key Finding
AAPL — Apple Negative 94.88% China -13%, iPad revenue -25%
MSFT — Microsoft Positive 95.37% Azure +28%, AI demand driving growth
GOOGL — Alphabet Positive 74.22% Cloud profitable, regulatory risks noted
AMZN — Amazon Positive 95.98% AWS reaccelerating, margins recovering
NVDA — NVIDIA Positive 95.72% Record revenue, +206% YoY growth

Why FinBERT over a general sentiment model? General models get financial language wrong. "Revenue was flat" sounds neutral but signals no growth — negative in finance. FinBERT was trained specifically on financial text and understands these nuances.

The Evolution

How it grew into something bigger

After the core NLP pipeline was working, the natural question was: does negative sentiment actually predict stock drops? Answering that required historical data, event detection, and machine learning. That's what the platform became.

Phase 1 — Extended

10 Companies, 10 Years of Data

Expanded from 5 to 10 companies — adding Tesla, Palantir, Snowflake, Datadog, and MongoDB alongside the original five. Pulled 10 years of daily stock price data via yfinance — 28,877 rows total — and stored everything in PostgreSQL.

Phase 2 — Earnings History

49 Quarters Per Company

Pulled up to 49 quarters of historical earnings data per company — EPS actual vs estimate, revenue, net income, and earnings surprise percentage. 454 total earnings records across all 10 companies.

Phase 3 — Event Detection

1,849 Major Price Events

Detected every single day where a stock moved more than 5% — 1,849 events total across all companies. TSLA had 350 major events (most volatile). MSFT had only 44 (most stable). Computed 1-day, 5-day, and 30-day price reactions after every earnings release.

Phase 4 — Prediction

ML Model for Next Quarter

Trained a GradientBoosting classifier on 435 labeled historical samples to predict next-quarter earnings sentiment. A separate regression model predicts the 30-day stock price reaction after earnings. Features include volatility, momentum, EPS surprise history, and revenue growth trends.

Phase 5 — Visualization

14 Interactive Charts

Built a full visualization layer using Plotly — normalized price comparison, individual price history with event annotations, earnings reaction heatmap, volatility comparison, and prediction summaries. All generated automatically and served via the portfolio demo page.
