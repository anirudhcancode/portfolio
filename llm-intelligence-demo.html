Project 02 — Extended

LLM Financial Intelligence Platform

An extension of the LLM Financial Analyzer — pulling 10 years of historical stock data for 10 companies, detecting major market events, analyzing earnings sentiment, and predicting next-quarter price reactions using machine learning.

What was built

PHASE 01

Historical Data Pipeline

10 years of daily OHLCV price data for 10 companies pulled via yfinance and stored in PostgreSQL. 28,877 rows total.

PHASE 02

Earnings History

Up to 49 quarters of earnings data per company — EPS actual vs estimate, revenue, net income, and surprise percentage.

PHASE 03

Event Detection & Analysis

Detected 1,849 major price events (>5% single-day moves) across all companies. Computed 1, 5, and 30-day price reactions after each earnings release.

PHASE 04

Predictive Model

GradientBoosting classifier trained on 435 labeled samples predicts next-quarter sentiment. Regression model predicts 30-day price reaction after earnings.

PHASE 05

API & Visualizations

9 FastAPI endpoints serving historical data, predictions, and correlation analysis. 14 interactive Plotly charts generated automatically.

Visualization 01

12-Month Performance Comparison

All 10 companies normalized to a common baseline of 100 — shows relative performance regardless of share price.

How to read this: Every company starts at 100 on the left side regardless of their actual stock price. If a company ends at 200, it doubled in value. If it ends at 50, it lost half its value. This lets you compare Apple ($200/share) and NVDA ($800/share) on equal footing — it's about growth, not price. The dashed line at 100 is the baseline — anything above it is a gain, anything below is a loss from where it started 12 months ago.

How to read this: The top panel shows the stock's closing price over 3 years. The ▲ green triangles mark days where the stock jumped more than 5% in a single day — usually triggered by strong earnings, product launches, or major news. The ▼ red triangles mark days where it dropped more than 5% — usually bad earnings, market crashes, or negative news.

The bottom panel shows 30-day rolling volatility — how "choppy" the stock has been recently. Higher volatility means bigger swings day to day. Lower volatility means steadier, more predictable movement. TSLA and PLTR tend to have much higher volatility than MSFT or AAPL.

Visualization 03

Earnings Reaction Heatmap

30-day price reaction after each earnings release. Green = stock went up, Red = stock went down.

How to read this: Each box represents one company's stock performance in the 30 days after their quarterly earnings report. Green means the stock went up, red means it went down, and the number shows by exactly how much.

For example, if NVDA shows +25% for Q3 2024, it means NVIDIA's stock was 25% higher 30 days after they released their Q3 earnings. Reading across a row shows how one company has reacted to earnings over time. Reading down a column shows how all companies reacted after the same quarter — useful for spotting market-wide trends.

Visualization 04

Volatility & Momentum

Which companies are most volatile? Which have the strongest recent momentum?

Volatility (left chart): Shows how much a stock's price swings day to day on average. High volatility means the stock moves a lot — exciting but risky. Low volatility means steadier, more predictable movement. TSLA and PLTR are typically the most volatile — they can move 5-10% in a single day. MSFT and AAPL tend to be the most stable.

Momentum (right chart): Shows how much the stock has moved in the last 30 days. Green bars mean the stock has been going up recently — positive momentum. Red bars mean it's been going down — negative momentum. This is useful for spotting which companies are currently trending in which direction.

Visualization 05

Next Quarter Predictions

ML model predictions for next quarter's earnings sentiment and 30-day price reaction. Trained on 435 labeled historical samples.

How to read this: These are machine learning predictions for what will happen after each company's next earnings report. The model was trained on 435 historical earnings events — it learned patterns between a company's recent volatility, momentum, and EPS surprise history to predict what comes next.

Left chart — Predicted 30-day price reaction: How much the model expects the stock to move in the 30 days after next earnings. A bar at +15% means the model predicts the stock will be 15% higher a month after earnings. Negative bars predict a decline.

Right chart — Sentiment confidence: How confident the model is in its prediction. 99% confidence means the historical patterns very strongly suggest a particular outcome. Lower confidence means the signals are mixed. These predictions are based purely on historical price and earnings patterns — not news, not fundamentals, not opinions.
